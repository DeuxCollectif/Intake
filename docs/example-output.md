## Executive Summary

The conceptual estimate risk posture is **moderate-to-high** at this stage, with primary exposure concentrated in market pricing volatility, live-campus productivity impacts, and scope definition maturity for complex MEP/clinical interfaces. Based on historical comparables in similar value bands and contract type, the likely risk direction is **upward on both cost and schedule** unless early design freeze discipline, procurement timing, and logistics planning are tightened before basis-of-estimate lock.

## Top Estimate Risks

### 1. Scope Definition Risk – Clinical Adjacency & Phased Turnover

**Risk Statement:** Scope definition risk in clinical adjacency and phased turnover requirements may drive late design change.

**Historical Evidence:** Similar healthcare expansions showed **3%–8% post-DD estimate growth** where phased occupancy criteria were not fully defined at concept stage.

**Likelihood:** HIGH | **Impact:** HIGH  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up, Schedule right

**Mitigation Action:**
- Set phased occupancy requirements by zone before 30% design complete
- Enforce design-change governance with executive escalation triggers
- Establish decision-rights matrix for clinical user groups

**Suggested Owner:** Clinical Liaison + Design Manager

---

### 2. Quantity & Takeoff Risk – Vertical Tie-Ins & Existing-Conditions Work

**Risk Statement:** Quantity and takeoff risk for vertical tie-ins and existing-conditions work may understate demolition, temporary works, and rework.

**Historical Evidence:** Projects with major tie-ins averaged **2%–5% quantity growth** from concept to IFC, especially in structural and MEP enabling packages.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** HIGH  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up

**Mitigation Action:**
- Add targeted early intrusive investigations (structural, MEP, utilities)
- Perform package-specific quantity validation (structure, above-ceiling MEP, temporary life safety)
- Include contingency for existing-building discovery findings

**Suggested Owner:** Estimating Manager + Building Science Lead

---

### 3. Market & Escalation Risk – Long-Lead Equipment

**Risk Statement:** Market and escalation risk remains elevated for electrical gear, controls, and medical gas components.

**Historical Evidence:** Recent healthcare projects in comparable markets absorbed **4%–9% procurement variance** tied to long-lead equipment timing.

**Likelihood:** HIGH | **Impact:** MEDIUM  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up, Schedule right

**Mitigation Action:**
- Pull forward long-lead procurement strategy before 25% design
- Prequalify alternates and document supplier capacity constraints
- Include escalation assumptions explicitly by commodity category
- Establish quarterly commodity price index monitoring

**Suggested Owner:** Procurement Lead + Estimator

---

### 4. Labor Availability & Productivity Risk – Regional Market & Active Campus

**Risk Statement:** Labor availability and productivity risk may reduce installed productivity due to regional demand and constrained working windows on active campus.

**Historical Evidence:** Sun Belt healthcare programs under active operations showed **8%–15% productivity drag** versus greenfield baselines.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** HIGH  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up, Schedule right

**Mitigation Action:**
- Validate labor loading assumptions by trade with current market data
- Add shift-premium scenario testing and crew-size sensitivity analysis
- Align shutdown windows with hospital operations earlier (pre-construction)
- Secure labor commitments from key trades by bid date

**Suggested Owner:** Construction Manager + Scheduling Specialist

---

### 5. Geolocation & Logistics Risk – Active-Campus Constraints

**Risk Statement:** Geolocation and logistics risk from active-campus constraints (access, infection control, noise windows) may increase indirects and sequencing complexity.

**Historical Evidence:** Campus projects with strict ICRA and access controls showed **1.5%–4% general conditions growth** and milestone slippage.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** MEDIUM  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up, Schedule right

**Mitigation Action:**
- Build logistics simulation into preconstruction phase
- Lock material routing plans and temporary staging areas
- Define contingency scenarios for controlled-access inefficiencies
- Coordinate with hospital infection prevention early

**Suggested Owner:** Project Manager + Logistics Coordinator

---

### 6. Contract & Commercial Risk – GMP Allowance Burn

**Risk Statement:** Contract and commercial risk under GMP may transfer less risk than assumed if clarifications and allowances are insufficiently defined.

**Historical Evidence:** GMP projects with immature bid packages saw allowance burn rates exceed plan by **20%–35%** in first half of construction.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** MEDIUM  
**Exposure Direction:** Cost up (through contingency/allowance consumption)

**Mitigation Action:**
- Tighten commercial assumptions in bid package narratives
- Establish early allowance governance with monthly burn-rate thresholds
- Define allowance triggers for design change vs. estimate miss
- Lock allowance assumptions for key trade packages by bid

**Suggested Owner:** Finance Manager + Commercial Manager

---

### 7. Schedule Compression Risk – Design Maturity vs. Construction Timeline

**Risk Statement:** Schedule compression risk is present if owner milestone expectations force overlap before design maturity.

**Historical Evidence:** Fast-tracked healthcare projects showed increased RFI/change velocity and **2–4 month completion pressure** when overlap exceeded planned tolerances.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** HIGH  
**Exposure Direction:** Schedule right, second-order cost up

**Mitigation Action:**
- Define non-negotiable design maturity gates before procurement release
- Maintain a risk-adjusted master schedule with float management
- Establish milestone review discipline and variance reporting
- Include contingency for accelerated decision-making costs

**Suggested Owner:** Scheduling Manager + Design Director

---

### 8. Stakeholder Decision Latency Risk – Multi-Stakeholder Governance

**Risk Statement:** Stakeholder decision latency risk may delay design closure across clinical user groups and facilities operations.

**Historical Evidence:** Multi-stakeholder healthcare programs with fragmented decision rights experienced recurring redesign cycles and **approval lag** averaging 2–3 weeks per decision point.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** MEDIUM  
**Exposure Direction:** Schedule right, indirect cost up

**Mitigation Action:**
- Implement a decision-rights matrix with clear authority levels
- Maintain a weekly decision log with SLA for escalation
- Executive escalation protocol for unresolved items
- Conduct kickoff with all stakeholder groups pre-design

**Suggested Owner:** Owner's Representative + Design Manager

---

### 9. Data Quality & Benchmark Relevance Risk – Comparator Normalization

**Risk Statement:** Data quality and benchmark relevance risk may bias early contingency if comparator normalization is incomplete.

**Historical Evidence:** Cross-project unit-rate comparisons without normalization (scope, phasing, geography, timing) produced **false confidence in early ROM positions**, leading to 5%–8% estimate surprises at later stages.

**Likelihood:** MEDIUM | **Impact:** MEDIUM  
**Exposure Direction:** Either direction, current bias toward underestimation

**Mitigation Action:**
- Normalize benchmark set with explicit adjustment factors (location, labor market, escalation period)
- Document confidence bounds for each comparator
- Validate outliers against project specifics
- Peer-review comparator selection with estimating leadership

**Suggested Owner:** Chief Estimator + Estimating Analyst

## Recommended Contingency Position

**Posture: Conservative-to-Moderate, with bias toward conservative until 30% design**

**Executive Rationale:**

Key risks are **not evenly distributed**; they are concentrated in known **high-impact drivers**:
- **Phasing complexity** and clinical adjacency definition
- **Long-lead procurement** volatility in regional market
- **Live-campus productivity** constraints and logistics complexity

These risks historically generate **asymmetric downside** when unresolved early. Current estimate is likely to experience upward movement during design development unless the following early decisions are locked:

1. **Phased occupancy requirements by zone** (by 30% design)
2. **Long-lead equipment procurement plan** (by 25% design)
3. **Campus logistics & sequencing framework** (by preconstruction)
4. **Allowance & contingency governance thresholds** (before contract execution)

**Contingency Recommendation:** Maintain a **conservative reserve** through 30% design; allow graduated reduction as design maturity and procurement clarity improve.

This regional hospital expansion presents a **manageable but moderately elevated risk profile** at concept stage. **Success depends on early closure** of three critical unknowns:

1. **Scope definition maturity** (clinical adjacency, phasing)
2. **Procurement strategy and long-lead timing**
